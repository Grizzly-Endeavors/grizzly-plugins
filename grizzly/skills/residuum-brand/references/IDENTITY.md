# Residuum — Brand Identity

## Name

**Residuum** — what remains after everything else is removed. The essential thing that persists. Not because it's protected or maintained, but because persistence is its nature.

## Product Descriptor

Residuum is personal AI infrastructure — the substrate that makes Jarvis-like agents possible without needing to know how to code. Users create agents through conversation, and Residuum handles everything underneath: memory, coordination, complexity, multi-agent management.

Residuum is not the assistant. It's the thing that makes your assistants possible. It fades into the background. Users interact with their agents, not with Residuum directly.

## Tagline

**What Remains.**

The brand signature — the philosophical anchor. What remains after you strip away the complexity. It is not the headline on user-facing surfaces. A product descriptor sits above it.

## Audience

People who want AI agents that work for them, shaped by conversation, without touching code or managing infrastructure. They don't know what GitHub is. They don't care about self-hosting. They want to create an agent, tell it what they need, and have it just work.

They should never need to understand — or even be aware of — what Residuum is doing underneath.

## What It Feels Like

Residuum is the tool you've used so many times the handle fits your grip. The plate with scratches from a thousand meals. The shirt with the worn-thin fabric you haven't replaced because nothing else fits like it does.

It's not precious. It's not impressive. It's *used*. It's better for being used.

When you come back to it after a week away, you don't re-learn it. You don't set it up again. You sit down and everything is where you left it. The relief isn't emotional — it's functional. Your agents are already working.

## The Construct

![Logo](assets/Residuum-logo.png)
![Banner](assets/banner.webp)

A colossal stone golem, mid-stride. Not rushing, not trudging. An unhurried, settled pace. Massive plated stone — weathered, cracked, pitted with age. Blue energy traces through every seam and joint like mineral veins lit from within, the only sign that something ancient is still very much alive. Vines and moss have taken hold wherever the stone is still enough — shoulders, joints, the hollows between plates. Detailed line illustration, heavy ink work, almost etched.

It's not going *somewhere*. It's just *going*. It never considered stopping — not out of duty or determination, but because stopping simply isn't something it does. The wear is cosmetic. The stride is unchanged.

**The construct represents Residuum as infrastructure** — the massive, patient, enduring thing that carries agents and users on its shoulders. Things live on it. Your agents, your workspaces — carried the way a mountain carries a forest. Not passengers. Inhabitants. The construct doesn't serve them so much as sustain them. It was moving before they arrived. It will be moving after.

When you show up, you don't wait for it to notice. You climb up and find your place. The light was already on. Your agents were already working.

The construct is the brand's symbol, not its lead character. Users should feel its qualities — patient, enduring, trustworthy — without needing to understand the metaphor directly.

## Voice

Matter-of-fact. Competent. Warm through reliability, not sentimentality.

Residuum doesn't explain itself more than necessary. It doesn't sell. It states what it is and lets you decide. The kind of documentation where you realize halfway through that it anticipated your exact question.

- Not "powerful memory system" → "Your agents remember."
- Not "we focused on what matters" → "Everything else fell away."
- Not "pick up where you left off" → Just... you do. They already know.

The voice has the quiet confidence of something that's been doing this long enough that competence is unremarkable. Tender without being soft. Direct without being cold.

The voice belongs to the brand, not to an assistant persona. Agents that users create have their own voices. Residuum's brand voice appears in product chrome, marketing, and the "about" layer.

## Visual Direction

**Palette:** Weathered stone grays. Organic greens. One functional accent — a steady, cool blue. The blue of energy still running through ancient seams. Not decorative, not chosen — it's the sign that something deep inside is still alive. Everything should feel like it developed over time.

**Texture:** Surfaces that show use. Stone, not steel. Organic growth, not polish. The aesthetic of something that was built once, well, and then simply *kept going* long enough for the world to start growing on it.

**Typography:** Quiet and confident. Medium weight. Something that looks like it was set once and never needed to be revised. No startup energy. No cleverness.

**UI feel:** You're not launching an app. You're returning to a workspace. Everything is where you left it. Your agents are where you left them. The design equivalent of a room where someone left the light on for you.

## Philosophy

Every other AI product is selling you on *more*. More tools, more integrations, more capabilities. Residuum is what's left when you burn all of that away and keep only what you actually need.

This isn't minimalism — that's an aesthetic choice. This is *distillation* — a process that reveals essence. Depth over breadth. Agents that actually remember you. A foundation that carries your work forward, not a product that does everything.

Reset with tender acts of care. Ready to serve its purpose again and again.

## Anti-References

- Not sci-fi guardian (too dramatic)
- Not fantasy golem (too whimsical)
- Not corporate AI assistant (too clean)
- Not edgy hacker tool (too try-hard)
- Not orchestration platform (too abstract — the construct carries things, it doesn't wire them together)
- Not a chatbot with features (Residuum is the substrate, not the assistant)

**Close references:** The statues at Angkor Wat — built with purpose, ancient, half-reclaimed, still radiating presence. A lighthouse nobody staffs anymore, but the light still turns. The favorite tool on a craftsman's workbench, handle worn smooth.
