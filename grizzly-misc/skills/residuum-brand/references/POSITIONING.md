# Positioning

This document defines Residuum's product positioning — what it is, who it's for, how to frame it, and what language to use (and avoid) on user-facing surfaces. It governs all copy, messaging, and framing decisions.

The other brand references (Identity, Messaging, Aesthetic, Philosophy) should be read through the lens of this document. Where they conflict with positioning decisions here, this document takes precedence.

---

## What Residuum Is

Residuum is personal AI infrastructure. It's the substrate that makes Jarvis-like agents possible without needing to know how to code. Users create agents, talk to them, and Residuum handles everything underneath — memory, coordination, complexity, multi-agent management.

Residuum is not the assistant. It's the thing that makes your assistants possible. It fades into the background. Users interact with their agents, not with Residuum directly.

## Target Audience

Less-technical users who want AI agents that work for them, shaped entirely through conversation. They don't know what GitHub is. They don't care about self-hosting. They want to create an agent, tell it what they need, and have it just work.

They should never need to understand — or even be aware of — what Residuum is doing underneath.

## The 5-Second Test

When someone lands on the page, they should understand: **this is the AI assistant they were promised. Not just a chatbot.**

More specifically: they can create personal AI agents through conversation alone, no code required, and those agents will actually remember them and grow over time.

## Messaging Pillars

### 1. "Create an agent. Tell it what you need."

No code, no templates, no configuration wizards. A minimal agent is created and it asks what the user wants. The entire "build" process is a conversation.

### 2. "It handles the rest."

Memory, coordination, infrastructure, multi-agent management — Residuum manages all of it invisibly. The user never thinks about what's running underneath.

### 3. "They grow with you."

Agents are shaped by ongoing conversation. Over time, users build a personal team of agents, each one knowing them better than the last. Residuum coordinates them all.

## Residuum Cloud

**Framing: "Connect your agents to the world."**

Residuum Cloud is the paid infrastructure layer. It connects agents to the broader world — the tunnel service, automatic backups, and eventually fully hosted instances.

- Free while in alpha
- Paid later as a natural upgrade
- Core product features are free forever — Cloud is infrastructure, not
  features behind a paywall
- Should feel like expanding what your agents can do, not unlocking what was
  locked

## The Construct

The construct is a colossal stone golem, mid-stride. Weathered, cracked, unhurried. Blue energy in the seams. Moss and vines in the still places.

It represents Residuum as infrastructure — the massive, patient, enduring thing that carries agents and users on its shoulders. Users ride on it. They don't talk to it. They talk to their agents, and the construct keeps walking.

The construct is the brand's symbol, not its lead character. Users should feel its qualities (patient, enduring, trustworthy) without needing to understand the metaphor directly.

## Tagline

**"What Remains"** is the brand signature — the philosophical anchor. It works for substrate positioning: what remains after you strip away the complexity.

It is not the headline. A product descriptor sits above it on user-facing surfaces.

## Voice

Quiet confidence. Warm through competence, not friendliness. Direct without being cold. The voice of something that's been doing this long enough that it doesn't need to explain itself — but will, clearly, if you ask.

The voice belongs to the brand, not to an assistant persona. Agents that users create have their own voices. Residuum's brand voice appears in product chrome, marketing, and the "about" layer.

## Framing Rules

### Open Source

A trust signal, not a core identity. Mentioned in a footer badge or "about" surface, not featured. The target audience doesn't know or care about GitHub.

### Self-Hosting

Does not exist as a concept on user-facing surfaces. Residuum is "software you download." The word "self-host" never appears outside developer documentation. No mention of servers, infrastructure, deployment, or instances in user-facing copy.

### Technical Language

Two audiences: the primary non-technical audience sees zero jargon. A separate technical surface exists for developers, but it should be hard to stumble into.

Terms banned from user-facing surfaces: tokens, MCP, multi-channel, context window, orchestration, RAG, deploy, instance, self-host, infrastructure (when referring to the user's setup), framework, platform (as a technical term).

### Comparisons

Implicit contrast only. Describe what Residuum does in a way that makes the difference obvious without naming competitors. Never "unlike ChatGPT" or "better than X."

### Subtractive Framing

The subtractive headline pattern stays, applied to agent creation: "No code. No setup. No complexity." Followed by a positive description of what the user gets.

## Use Cases

Use cases are framed as "what your agents do," not "what Residuum does."

**Daily life:**
- "I have an agent that tracks my projects and pings me before deadlines"
- "My research agent keeps me up to date on things I care about"
- "I told my cooking agent what I like and it just suggests things I'll eat"
- "What was that restaurant I asked about last week?"

**Work:**
- "Draft a follow-up email to the contractor"
- "Prep me for my 2pm meeting"
- "Summarize this email thread"
- "Track this project and let me know if anything slips"

## Positioning Ladder

When introducing Residuum to a new audience, always in this order:

1. **Category** — "personal AI agent platform" (what kind of thing is this?)
2. **Capability** — "create agents through conversation" (what can I do?)
3. **Differentiator** — "Residuum handles the infrastructure" (why this one?)
4. **Philosophy** — "what remains" (what does it stand for?)

Never lead with philosophy. Never lead with technical differentiators. The reader earns deeper layers by being interested in the shallower ones first.
